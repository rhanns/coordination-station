Mitzvah Garden: A Vision for Transformation

Our Values: Tikkun HaAdamah

The Back to Eden method comes from a Christian framework, and while we give credit to that origin, our work here is rooted in Jewish values. We're calling our approach Tikkun HaAdamah‚Äîrepairing the soil‚Äîbecause this is about tikkun olam (repairing the world) at the most fundamental level: healing the ground beneath our feet.

Bal tashchit (do not destroy) calls us to stop wasting‚Äîno more plastic in landfills, no more depleting the soil. Tzedek, tzedek tirdof (justice, justice you shall pursue) means ensuring our most vulnerable community members have access to truly nourishing food, not just empty calories. And there's deep wisdom in letting the land rest and regenerate‚Äîwe're learning to work with creation instead of exhausting it.

The Soil Reality

Our soil is pretty much dead right now. Years of tilling and covering with plastic have left us with dirt that has almost no life in it. No worms, minimal microbes, poor structure. We've been repeating the same mistakes as modern industrial agriculture‚Äîfighting the soil, depleting it, treating it like dirt instead of a living system. Dead soil grows depleted food‚Äîthe vegetables might look fantastic, but they're missing the vitamins and minerals people actually need.

But here's our advantage: we're gardeners, not farmers. We don't need tractors or wide rows or chemical inputs. We have the opportunity to work with the soil and creation, not against it. The mulch-and-compost system we're transitioning to will wake the soil back up. This winter, we're mulching everything heavily so it composts in place by spring. Here's the critical part: the intensive square-foot spacing we're implementing will only work with living soil. You can't pack plants that densely into dead dirt. But in soil alive with microbes, fungi, and worms? Those organisms feed the plants and make intensive production possible. We get to do this right.

Year 2026: Full Implementation and Setup

Picture this: All the beds are finally marked and measured. No more tilling (which moves the beds from season to season), no more wrestling with torn plastic sheeting stuck in the soil. The wood chip mulch is thick on every bed, green clover is spreading through the pathways. Cattle panels are standing vertical, ready for vines to climb. The software knows every bed measurement and volunteers are getting clear weekly schedules down to the exact number of seeds to start.

The soil is starting to wake up‚Äîyou can spot worms when you plant now. Water use is already dropping because the mulch holds moisture. The garden looks intentional, professional, organized. When those spring rains come? The mulch soaks it up. No mud, no waiting for the ground to dry so we can till. We just keep working.

Year 2027: Soil Biology Activates

The garden is transforming. You can see the difference in the soil now‚Äîit's fluffy, dark, alive. Earthworms are everywhere. The mulch from last year has composted down beautifully into rich, dark soil and is replenished with fresh material to continue the process. The clover pathways are fully established and lush, pumping nitrogen into the ground.

The cattle panel trellises are loaded with cucumbers and squash hanging at perfect harvest height‚Äîno more crawling around on hands and knees. Weeding? Almost none. The mulch and clover have it covered. We're using half the rainwater we used to need, reducing our dependence on the Temple water hose during drought. The plants look healthier‚Äîdeeper green, more vigorous‚Äîbecause the soil biology is feeding them. You can taste the difference in the food.

The soil stays cooler in the summer heat and warmer as fall hits‚Äîthat biological activity is regulating temperature. We're testing hoop houses over a few beds to see if we can grow through winter. Weather isn't dictating our schedule anymore.

Year 2028 and Beyond: The Fungal Highway

Walk through the garden now and it's hard to believe this is the same space. Three years of biological activity have created a soil ecosystem that runs itself. And here it is‚Äîthis was my secret plan, my ultimate goal all along: underground, mycorrhizal fungal networks spreading across the entire garden connect the plant roots like a living highway system, pulling nitrogen from the clover pathways and delivering it directly to the crops in the beds. The clover is fixing nitrogen from the air, the fungi are trading it to the vegetables, and we're just... watching it happen. We're not fertilizing anymore. The system fertilizes itself.

And when it rains? The water percolates through the composting mulch layer, creating a nutrient-rich tea that soaks into the soil. The earthworms and soil organisms distribute it everywhere as they move through their tunnels and burrows. Every rainfall is a feeding event. Every bit of biology is working together‚Äîfungi, bacteria, worms, insects‚Äîall cycling nutrients exactly where the plants need them. This is the perfect environment we've been building toward.

The soil temperature stays moderated year-round‚Äîwarmer in winter, cooler in summer. The hoop houses worked‚Äîwe're producing food year-round now, greens and root vegetables all winter long. The produce coming out of these beds is packed with nutrients‚Äîyou can see it in the color, taste it in the flavor. Vitamins and minerals that store-bought produce just doesn't have anymore. Our community members are eating food that actually nourishes them.

The vertical trellises are producing more per square foot than we ever imagined. Labor is at an all-time low‚Äîno weeding, no tilling, no plastic hassles, no waiting around for dry weather, no fertilizing. We're using a fraction of the rainwater we used to. Disease and pest pressure are naturally down because healthy soil grows healthy plants. We're producing 3-4 times what we used to, more food than if we traditionally farmed land twice this size. The garden is feeding itself and feeding our community.

ü§ù Mitzvah Garden Volunteer Squads (Work in Progress)

MGKC operates through a flexible, squad-based volunteer system designed to make Sunday work sessions accessible, organized, and enjoyable for volunteers of all skill levels. Rather than rigid assignments, volunteers can choose which squad to join each week based on their interests, energy level, and what sounds appealing.

How Our Squad System Works

Each squad is led by a Team Captain‚Äîa regular volunteer selected by the MGKC Director who has demonstrated relevant knowledge and can effectively guide groups of 5-10 volunteers. Team Captains are responsible for planning their squad's Sunday activities, either independently or in coordination with the Farm Coordinator, and ensuring their crew has clear direction and meaningful work.

The level of autonomy varies by squad: the Bloom Squad has significant creative freedom in designing ornamental plantings, the Garden Gang follows predetermined planting schedules managed through our garden planning software, and The Wild Things operate with substantial independence in forest cultivation projects‚Äîthough final approval from the MGKC Director is required for all species removal or introduction.

üå∏ Bloom Squad

Led by the Captain of Beautification

The Bloom Squad maintains our ornamental gardens, flower beds, and pollinator-friendly plantings. This crew manages Lover's Lane‚Äîour extensive butterfly weed pathway established with hundreds of plants through a KU grant and local student assistance‚Äîalong with seasonal color rotations and habitat creation for beneficial insects. Activities include maintaining Lover's Lane, deadheading spent blooms, mulching perennials, and identifying opportunities for new beautification projects. The Bloom Squad keeps our community space vibrant and welcoming throughout the seasons.

ü•¨ Garden Gang

Led by the Farm Coordinator and assisted by our Sunday regulars

The Garden Gang manages our fenced vegetable garden and wood chip bed systems. This crew handles seasonal planting, weeding, harvesting, and ongoing maintenance of our growing beds. We organize by Crop Captains‚Äîvolunteers who take ownership of a specific crop (tomatoes, okra, greens, etc.) and commit to weekly maintenance. Whether you're interested in pruning tomato suckers, managing the okra harvest, or tending salad greens, the Garden Gang ensures our community has fresh, homegrown produce throughout the growing season. Activities range from preparing soil amendments to succession planting and daily harvest coordination.

üçé Tree Team

Led by our Orchard Steward

The Tree Team is a smaller, specialized crew that oversees our fruit orchard, including pruning, pest management, harvesting, and propagation projects. This crew works with established fruit trees and develops new plantings through grafting and layering techniques. Major annual event: Our comprehensive fig maintenance day, which involves mulching hundreds of plants, harvesting leaves for teas and extracts, and conducting heavy pruning. Activities include learning proper pruning techniques for various fruit trees, propagating fig cuttings, and cultivating our long-term fruit production systems.

üå≤ The Wild Things

Led by the Food Forest Steward ‚Ä¢ Requires experienced adult supervision

‚ö†Ô∏è Safety Requirements: The Food Forest Steward must have demonstrated expertise in plant identification and foraging safety. This crew works with potentially hazardous plants including poison ivy and invasive species. Proper identification protocols and safety equipment are mandatory. All activities involving foraging education require verification by a qualified expert.

The Wild Things focus on building a productive food forest in the land surrounding MGKC‚Äîcreating a woodland ecosystem that yields thousands of pounds of edible nuts, berries, and seeds annually. This crew tackles invasive species removal, foraging education (under expert guidance), native plant cultivation, and the long-term establishment of a diverse perennial food system. Activities include identifying edible plants and mushrooms, managing invasive Bradford pears, planting berry bushes in the understory, and developing sustainable woodland cultivation practices that bridge wilderness and intentional agriculture.

üìö The Learning Garden

Managed by the Summer Intern under MGKC Director supervision

The Learning Garden coordinates our high school volunteer program, greenhouse operations, and community tours. This crew of dedicated teen volunteers manages weekday greenhouse maintenance, serves as liaisons with our partner school, and facilitates educational experiences for visiting groups. Adult supervision required: All youth groups must be accompanied by a responsible adult chaperone. The Learning Garden ensures our space serves as a living classroom while developing the next generation of environmental stewards and community leaders.

üå± Sprout Crew

Managed by the Summer Intern under MGKC Director supervision

The Sprout Crew welcomes ma'asim tovim campers, middle schoolers, Sunday school students, and visiting youth groups. Adult supervision required: All groups must be accompanied by a responsible adult chaperone. Under guidance from experienced volunteers, Sprouts gain hands-on experience across all garden areas‚Äîplanting seeds, pulling weeds, harvesting vegetables, and learning where food comes from. The Sprout Crew brings energy, curiosity, and joy to our Sunday sessions while building foundational connections to food, nature, and community service.

Join a Squad

No long-term commitment required‚Äîsimply arrive on Sunday mornings and choose which crew sounds most interesting that day. Every squad welcomes volunteers of all experience levels, and every contribution makes a meaningful difference. Whether you're an experienced gardener or have never worked with soil before, there's a place for you at Mitzvah Garden.

Goal

Line Ben Yehuda access road with 500 Chicago Hardy fig trees to create food production corridor and visual landmark. By planting 500 trees to target 10,000 lbs annual production, we build in resilience against die-off, harsh winters, and natural losses.

Why Figs?

  • Thrive on neglect and inconsistent watering
  • Drought-tolerant once established - minimal watering needed
  • No special soil requirements
  • 75-85% success rate propagating from cuttings - can make 250+ clones from existing trees
  • Hardy to Zone 6, die back in winter but regrow from roots each spring
  • Mature Chicago Hardy figs yield 20-25 lbs per tree annually when properly pruned
  • Resilience through numbers: Even with 10-15% tree loss, we still hit production targets

The Food Access Angle

Fresh figs retail $4-6/lb but are nearly impossible to find in grocery stores because they don't ship well‚Äîthey're too delicate. Food pantries never get figs. This is luxury produce that low-income families have zero access to. We're creating a pipeline for high-value, nutritionally dense fruit that would otherwise never reach food insecure communities.

The Resilience Strategy

We're planting 500 trees to produce 10,000 lbs‚Äîwhich means each mature tree only needs to produce 20 lbs annually (well below their 25-30 lb potential). This gives us a massive buffer:

  • Winter die-back: Even a brutal winter that kills 75-100 trees still leaves us at target production
  • Disease/pest pressure: If a section gets hit, the rest carry the load
  • Continuous replanting: We propagate 50-75 replacement trees annually to fill gaps
  • Natural attrition: 10-15% expected losses are already baked into the plan

Distribution Impact

At full production (2031+), 10,000 lbs feeds approximately:

  • 2,500 families (4 lbs each) OR
  • 5,000 individuals (2 lbs each)

Harvest window: August-October, 6-8 weeks of weekly distribution

Retail value equivalent: $40,000-60,000 (at $4-6/lb retail) annually in produce we're giving away for free

üè° Mitzvah Garden at Home

A blueprint for practicing Tikkun HaAdamah in your own space - we'll teach you the method, and you can load up buckets of compost and leaves when you volunteer.

The Philosophy

You don't need 50 beds and 500 fig trees to practice Tikkun HaAdamah. Repairing the soil is repairing the world - and that starts wherever you are.

This is a blueprint for how to apply MGKC's methods at home. Same wood chip mulch. Same leaf compost. Same living soil principles. Same Tikkun HaAdamah approach. We'll teach you the system, you source the materials.

Every tomato plant, every handful of compost, every earthworm is an act of tikkun olam. Start small. Start now. We'll help you get there.

According to the (2009) National Gardening Association, the average home vegetable garden yields about $600 worth of produce while costing only $70 to maintain‚Äîthat's an 8:1 return on investment. For a community garden feeding local food pantries, this means every dollar our volunteers put into their own gardens amplify their household savings, their overall nutrition, and we will GLADLY take your excess to be distributed to our community.

Overview & Strategy

We grow three types of beans for different pantry needs: pole beans for continuous fresh harvest along our fence line, bush beans for fresh green beans in beds, and dry beans for shelf-stable protein. This combination provides both immediate fresh produce and long-term food security.

Pole Beans (Fresh)

Location: 148ft fence line
Spacing: 2 plants per linear foot
Total: ~296 plants
Yield: 5-8 lbs per plant = 1,480-2,368 lbs

Bush Beans (Fresh)

Location: 4 sq ft at end of 25 rows
Spacing: 9 plants per sq ft
Total: ~900 plants
Yield: 0.5-1 lb per plant = 450-900 lbs

Dry Beans (Storage)

Option: Convert half bush beans to dry
Plants: ~450 for dry beans
Yield: 0.25-0.5 lbs per plant = ~157 lbs dried
Impact: 700-800 cups shelf-stable protein

Care & Maintenance

Watering

1 inch per week, consistent moisture. CRITICAL during flowering and pod development. Mulch to retain moisture.

Fertilizing

Beans fix their own nitrogen! Light compost at planting is enough. Don't over-fertilize or you'll get leaves, not beans.

Pest Management

Watch for Mexican bean beetles and aphids. Hand-pick beetles. Neem oil for aphids if needed. Good airflow prevents disease.

Harvest Tips

Pick in morning when pods are crisp. For fresh beans: harvest young and tender. For dry: let fully mature on plant.

Food Pantry Notes

  • Fresh beans: Best donated within 24-48 hours of harvest. Keep cool and dry.
  • Dried beans: Store in sealed containers. Label with variety and harvest date. Shelf life of 1-2 years.
  • Presentation: Fresh beans look great in baskets. Dried beans can be portioned into bags with cooking instructions.
  • Why both? Fresh provides immediate nutrition. Dried provides long-term protein and food security.
  • Value: Dry beans are MORE valuable per pound to pantries than fresh equivalent!
